 Posted on August 5, 2024  |  20 minutes  |  4110 words  |  Reinaldo Massengill

Hand cream may sound like an ordinary, and perhaps even unnecessary, product to keep on hand. But we’re here to tell you that finding the best hand cream is kind of a big deal. Over the course of an average day, our hands go through a lot, from typing away at computers to frequent hand washing or rubbing a dollop of alcohol-based hand sanitizer into our palms, and after taking a look at our parched, tight skin, we knew we needed to find the best hand creams on the market. 

We tested 31 of the most popular hand creams and lotions over a two week span. We evaluated the overall look and feel of our hands after each use, including the level of hydration the cream offered and how easily it absorbed into the skin. As busy folks in the working world, we also paid attention to how much time passed before more lotion was needed to soothe our hands. Only the hand creams that delivered across the board made our list.

What to Keep in Mind

Ingredients

Just as when buying a day or night face cream, when shopping for the best hand creams, it’s important to pay close attention to the various ingredients in each product. You’ll notice that many of the mentioned products contain shea butter, which is useful as it's a natural ingredient rich in fatty acids and anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, explains Dr. LoGerfo. “Shea butter has the ability to eliminate superficial irritation and redness. Vitamin F, which is found in shea butter, can also improve the skin barrier to moisturize, and heal the dermis.” This nutty ingredient can be found in the L'OCCITANE Shea Butter Hand Cream, Benton Shea Butter and Olive Hand Cream, and Jergens Lavender Body Butter Body and Hand Lotion.

Ceramides are another key element to fortify the skin barrier. It does its best work in heavy-duty creams, like the EltaMD So Silky Hand Crème, says New York City-based board certified dermatologist Dr. Claire Wolinsky. She adds that other ingredients, including petrolatum "helps reduce water loss, while glycerin and  hyaluronic acid are all great moisturizing ingredients." Petrolatum can be found in the Vichy Ultra-Nourishing Hand Cream and Jergens Lavender Body Butter Body and Hand Lotion, while glycerin is found in the Fenty Skin Hydra'Reset Intensive Recovery Glycerin Hand Mask.

Texture

Ultimately, the goal is for your hands to simply feel better and softer after applying a hand cream. However, different skin types will need different types of nourishment and ingredients via a range of creamy textures. Those looking for preventative care might prefer lightweight formulas that feel thin and glide over the skin like melted butter, like Benton Shea Butter and Olive Hand Cream or Nécessaire's Hand Cream. People with cracked or excessively dry skin should opt for a heavy-duty cream with a thicker consistency, like the Skinfix Hand Repair Cream. 

Scent

Scented hand creams can act as a subtle and sweet midday treat to your nose, particularly if the aroma is known for therapeutic properties, like the lavender in the Jergens Lavender Body Butter Body and Hand Lotion.

However, many may want to opt for creams without any added fragrance, just for the sake of avoiding potential irritants. The Grown Alchemist Hand Cream Vanilla Orange Peel, for example, smells naturally fresh, courtesy of the orange peel extract, while the Sol de Janeiro Brazilian Touch Hand Cream leans on the cupuaçu butter to produce a mild vanilla-like aroma.

Generally speaking, Dr. Wolinsky advises those with sensitive skin or noses to reach for fragrance-free options entirely, like the Skinfix Hand Repair Cream. 

Your Questions, Answered

Is hand cream different from body lotion? 

Yes. Overall, hand creams contain a higher concentration of hydrating oils or other more potent ingredients with strong moisturizing properties than body lotions, which tend to be watery.

While body lotions work with the natural oils our bodies already create, hand lotions need to provide that moisture. Board certified dermatologist Dr. Kseniya Kobets who practices in Westchester County, New York, explains that while our bodies produce natural sebum to protect our skin from dehydration, “the palms of our hands and soles of our feet are the only places on our body that do not have sebaceous glands."

She says this can be especially tricky for hands, as they are constantly “interacting with irritants like soaps, detergents, and hand sanitizers, thus getting weathered even further," making hands more vulnerable to drying out than the rest of our bodies. As a result, hand creams are designed to provide extra hydration specifically for this area, which other parts of the body might find a bit too thick or rich.

How often should you apply hand cream? 

“The frequency with which you should apply hand cream is determined by how good your skin barrier function is,” explains Dr. Kobets. If your hands look and feel dry, you have eczema, or are in a profession that is very hands-on (literally), then she recommends moisturizing at least two to three times a day. 

Dr. Wolinsky chimes in that “every time you wash your hands, you should apply hand cream to restore the skin barrier, particularly since when the skin is damp, moisturizers will be absorbed better.”

Other lotions that work more like a mask (take the Fenty Skin Hydra'Reset Intensive Recovery Glycerin Hand Mask, for example) can be applied once before sleep and should keep your skin well hydrated for the day.
